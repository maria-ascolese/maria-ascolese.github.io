How to Measure the Unmeasurable

You cannot put a sensor on trust. You cannot directly observe satisfaction, quality, or intelligence. But you can measure things that reliably track them. Proxy variables are the most creative tool in data science — and the most misused.

Every data project eventually runs into the same wall: the thing you actually care about cannot be measured directly. You want to understand customer satisfaction, but satisfaction is a mental state. You want to track product quality, but quality is a judgment. You want to measure the effectiveness of a political message, but effectiveness lives in the minds of people who haven't told you anything yet.

This is not a failure of data. It is the normal condition of working with data on anything that matters. The concepts worth measuring are almost always too complex, too private, or too intangible to observe directly. What you can observe are traces — behaviors, signals, side effects that correlate with the thing you care about strongly enough to serve as a stand-in. These are called proxy variables. And choosing the right one is less a statistical decision than a creative one.

What a proxy variable actually is

A proxy variable is any measurable quantity used in place of something that cannot be measured directly. The formal definition from the SAGE Encyclopedia of Social Science Research Methods is precise: "a variable that is used to measure an unobservable quantity of interest." A good proxy is strongly correlated with the target concept. A bad proxy looks correlated but actually measures something else, or measures the right thing in the wrong conditions.

The concept is everywhere in data science, often without the name. GDP as a proxy for wellbeing. IQ scores as a proxy for intelligence. Completion rate as a proxy for content quality. Return rate as a proxy for product satisfaction. Every time you use a measurable signal to stand in for an unmeasurable concept, you are using a proxy — whether you call it that or not.

Concept → proxy → what you actually measure

Three cases worth examining closely

Netflix: the dropout point

Netflix cannot directly measure whether you enjoyed a film. Enjoyment happens inside a viewer's mind. But Netflix can measure where you stopped watching, and what you did next.

According to Netflix's own engineering writing on their recommendation systems, the company treats behavioral signals — completion rate, time to abandon, whether you returned to a title after pausing, whether you watched more content in the same genre afterward — as proxies for satisfaction. A viewer who finishes a season in a single day is a strong satisfaction signal. A viewer who abandons a film after ten minutes is ambiguous: they might have been interrupted, or they might have given up. The point of abandonment matters. Dropping out at the beginning reads differently from dropping out at the midpoint of an episode you've been watching for weeks.

The sophistication is in reading these signals together, not individually. No single behavior is a clean proxy. But a pattern of behaviors — start, pause, return, complete, explore similar content — gives a picture of engagement that correlates well with what people actually report when asked directly.

Amazon: returns as a quality signal

Amazon tracks return rates at the product level as a proxy for quality. The logic is direct: if a significant fraction of buyers return a product, something about it did not meet expectations — whether because the product is genuinely defective, because the listing was misleading, or because the product-market fit was wrong.

Amazon's internal metric system distinguishes between types of returns — including what it calls NCX (Not as Customer Expected) returns, which specifically flag cases where the product did not match the buyer's reasonable expectations based on the listing. A high NCX return rate is a proxy for a specific kind of quality failure: not that the product is bad in absolute terms, but that it was misrepresented. That distinction is analytically meaningful and leads to different remedies.

GDP: the proxy that ate the concept

Gross domestic product was designed to measure economic output. Over time it became a proxy for national wellbeing, prosperity, and the success of governments. This is a textbook example of a proxy that outgrew its original purpose.

GDP does not measure whether people are healthy, educated, safe, or satisfied with their lives. It does not distinguish between spending that creates value and spending that repairs damage. A country recovering from a natural disaster shows rising GDP. A country with high inequality may show strong GDP growth while median living standards stagnate.

The problem is not that GDP is a bad measure of output — it is a fine measure of output. The problem is that it became the default answer to questions it was never designed to answer. This happens when a proxy becomes so entrenched that people forget it is a proxy.

The danger is not choosing the wrong proxy. The danger is forgetting that you chose one at all.

What makes a good proxy

Not all proxies are equal. The choice of proxy is both a technical and a creative decision — and getting it wrong can lead an entire organization to optimize for the wrong thing. Here are the criteria I apply when evaluating a proxy variable in practice.

01
Genuine correlation
The proxy must actually track the concept of interest across a range of conditions, not just in the data you first observed it in. Validate on held-out data.
02
Correct direction
More of the proxy should mean more (or less) of the concept, consistently. A proxy that reverses direction under certain conditions is a liability.
03
Not gameable
Once a proxy becomes a target, people optimize for it directly. A good proxy should be hard to inflate without actually improving the underlying concept.
04
Interpretable failure modes
You should be able to describe the specific conditions under which the proxy breaks down. If you can't, you don't understand it well enough to use it.

The third criterion deserves extra attention. It is a restatement of Goodhart's Law: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Return rate is a reasonable proxy for product quality — until sellers discover they can reduce return rates by making returns harder. Completion rate is a reasonable proxy for content quality — until content is designed to be compulsive rather than valuable. The proxy holds until the system learns to optimize for it directly.

The creative part

The technical requirements of a good proxy — correlation, stability, interpretability — are necessary but not sufficient. The harder part is imagination: looking at a concept you want to measure and asking, genuinely openly, what traces it leaves in the world that you could collect.

Urban planners trying to measure the vitality of a neighborhood might look at the number of lit windows at 10pm. Epidemiologists trying to track disease prevalence before test results are available look at search query patterns. Researchers trying to measure economic activity in real time look at satellite images of commercial parking lots and port shipping volume. None of these are obvious. All of them required someone to stop asking "how do I measure this thing" and start asking "what does this thing leave behind?"

That shift in framing is, I think, what distinguishes useful data work from merely competent data work. The tools and methods are learnable. The willingness to sit with a hard measurement problem and ask genuinely creative questions about it — that is what turns a dataset into an insight.

Data does not contain truth. It contains traces. The work is learning to read them.
