When the Data Reveals the Unexpected

Some of the most powerful discoveries in data science happen when the numbers reveal something about human behavior that nobody thought to look for. These are my favorite kind.

There is a particular kind of satisfaction that comes from a finding nobody asked for. You are looking at data for one reason — customer satisfaction, sales forecasting, supply chain optimization — and the numbers show you something else entirely. Something about how people actually behave, as opposed to how they say they do, or how we assumed they would.

These discoveries don't come from hypothesis testing. They come from being genuinely curious about the residuals: the variance that your model doesn't explain, the pattern that shouldn't be there. The question underneath all of them is the same: what else is going on?

Here are three cases I keep coming back to. Each one surprised the people who found it. Each one, in retrospect, makes complete sense.

Case 1: Rain makes your reviews worse

Finding 01

Hotel guests rate their stay lower when it's raining — at home, weeks later.

6.5% more reviews written on rainy days · ratings 0.1 stars lower on average

Research by Yaniv Dover (Hebrew University of Jerusalem) and Leif Brandes (University of Lucerne), based on 340,000 hotel reviews linked to 12 years of weather data, found that people are significantly more likely to write a review on a rainy day — and when they do, they rate their past experience lower. The weather during the hotel stay itself had no effect. The weather on the day they sat down to write the review did.

This is a result that would have been invisible to anyone analyzing satisfaction scores alone. You'd see variance. You'd assume it was noise. It is only when you join the review data to an external dataset — in this case, historical weather records — that the pattern emerges.

The mechanism is well-documented in psychology: bad weather activates negative memories and frames the emotional context in which we recall experiences. We are not rating the hotel. We are rating the hotel as recalled from a rainy Tuesday afternoon. The 0.1-star difference is enough, on average, to account for 59% of the rating gap between four- and five-star hotels in the dataset.

The practical implication is uncomfortable for any business that relies on review scores as a quality signal: your ratings are partly a function of the weather in your customers' cities on the day they happened to open the review platform. You cannot control this. But you can stop treating your average score as a pure measure of quality.

Case 2: A hurricane is about to hit — stock up on Pop-Tarts

Finding 02

Before Hurricane Frances in 2004, Walmart discovered that strawberry Pop-Tart sales rose 7x ahead of a storm.

7× normal sales rate for strawberry Pop-Tarts pre-hurricane · beer was the #1 pre-storm item overall

Walmart's then-chief information officer Linda Dillman described the finding to the New York Times: by mining historical sales data across recent hurricane events, the company found that before a storm, customers reliably bought strawberry Pop-Tarts at seven times the normal rate. Nobody predicted this. No marketing team planned for it. The data found it.

The logic is clear once you see it: Pop-Tarts don't require refrigeration, don't need cooking, are high-calorie, and last indefinitely. They are, functionally, a perfect emergency food. But nobody would have predicted this from first principles. A category manager thinking about hurricane preparation would have thought about water, batteries, canned goods. Not a children's breakfast pastry.

What Walmart did next is the interesting part. They loaded trucks with strawberry Pop-Tarts and sent them toward the path of Hurricane Frances before it hit. They had turned a pattern in historical data into a logistical decision. This is what it looks like when data discovery becomes operational.

Nobody predicted this from first principles. The data found it. And once found, it was immediately useful.

The broader point is about the shape of useful questions in retail analytics. The obvious questions — what sells most, what has the highest margin, what's trending — all assume you already know which products matter. The less obvious question — under what conditions does the composition of a shopping basket change in predictable, non-intuitive ways — is harder to ask but reveals something you can actually act on.

Case 3: Sunshine moves stock markets

Finding 03

Market returns are systematically lower on cloudy days — across multiple countries and decades of data.

Sunshine effect documented across US, UK, Germany, Japan, and 22 other markets · effect too small to profit from, but statistically robust

A growing body of research in behavioral finance, including work published in the Journal of Finance and later replicated across 26 international markets, finds that stock market returns are measurably higher on sunny days than cloudy ones. The effect is small — not enough to build a trading strategy around — but it is persistent, it survives controls for seasonality and other confounds, and it appears across geographies and time periods.

The mechanism is the same as the rain-and-reviews case: mood shapes judgment, and ambient conditions shape mood. Traders in a better mood are marginally more optimistic, slightly more risk-tolerant, and a little more likely to buy. Aggregated across millions of transactions, the signal becomes measurable.

What this finding exposes is the gap between the model of markets as rational price-discovery mechanisms and the reality of markets as the aggregate behavior of human beings sitting at desks, looking out windows, having good days and bad ones. The efficient market hypothesis has no variable for cloud cover. The data does.

What these three cases share

None of these findings came from someone asking the right question. They came from someone looking at the data without a fixed hypothesis and noticing something that didn't fit the expected pattern. This is different from exploratory data analysis in the conventional sense — it's not just running correlations until something comes up. It requires a certain posture: genuine curiosity about what the data is trying to tell you, rather than what you already believe it should say.

All three also share the same underlying structure: a human behavior (writing a review, buying groceries, placing a trade) that we treat as if it were purely rational, and that turns out to be significantly shaped by context we weren't measuring. The rain, the hurricane, the sunshine. None of these are in the model. All of them affect the outcome.

The most useful data projects I've worked on have had this quality: at some point, something in the analysis surprises everyone in the room. That surprise is a signal. It means the data contains more than your prior assumptions did. That gap — between what you expected and what you found — is often where the actual value is.
